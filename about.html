About

From Marine Aviator to Product Executive

A career built at the intersection of strategy, technology, operations, and leadership.

Jamison Shaver - San Francisco Bay Area

Over the past 26 years, my career has centered on helping organizations navigate technological change by operating at the intersection of strategy, technology, operations, and leadership.

I began my career in the Marine Corps as a CH-53E helicopter pilot, where I learned the value of technical knowledge, disciplined process, and clear decision-making under pressure. Over two deployments supporting combat operations, I learned how to operate in complex, dynamic environments where information is incomplete, priorities shift quickly, and success depends on aligning people around a mission and executing with discipline.

I transitioned to industry through a rotational leadership development program at GE designed for military veterans. There, I discovered that the decisions shaping the future of a business were being made in Product Management, and soon had the opportunity to launch a new software business expanding GE into Smart Grid and Energy markets through early Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) technologies. I spent four years growing that product line while earning my MBA at night, and indirectly led a business model transformation with GE's first SaaS offering.

That success brought me to the Bay Area, where I was drawn to environments where technology was reshaping markets faster and I could work closer to customers, at startup speed. Most of that time was spent at Nstream (formerly Swim), where I joined as the company's first product hire and helped commercialize its initial product while aligning leadership around an open-source platform strategy that accelerated enterprise developer adoption.

Most recently at EagleView, I helped lead the company's evolution from a traditional services business toward an AI-powered geospatial intelligence platform - launching multiple new products while transitioning to recurring software and data revenue, delivering double-digit growth and positioning the portfolio for future expansion.

Throughout, I've learned that organizations succeed under different constraints, which means adapting principles rather than relying on a single playbook. And technology alone doesn't create competitive advantage - advantage comes from recognizing where technology creates opportunity, then having the discipline to transform the product portfolio, business model, and organization to capitalize on it.

How I Think About the Work

Philosophy

Executive Philosophy

In the Marine Corps, I learned that plans rarely survive first contact with reality. I've spent my career since at that intersection - helping organizations build the products, models, and teams to navigate uncertainty, whether they're finding their footing or evolving beyond what made them successful.

Leadership Philosophy

People consistently achieve more than they believe possible when they understand the mission, have the right support, and are empowered to solve difficult problems. My job is to create that environment.

Product Philosophy

Having worked across Fortune 100, venture-backed startups, and private equity-backed companies, I've learned that good product decisions are rarely about applying a playbook - they're about exercising sound judgment and applying frameworks within the realities of a particular business.

Market Philosophy

Despite the best business plans, markets ultimately decide whether a product succeeds. I've learned as much from failure as from success - and that's taught me when to stop, pivot, or persevere.

Judgment in Practice

At Eagleview

Resources historically focused on maintaining existing products and tech debt - a pattern that led to flat revenue growth. The first judgment call was to redirect up to 60% of engineering capacity toward growth and focus on launching the company's first major new product in years.

That required reorganizing the product team around customer outcomes rather than internal processes. We established a disciplined evaluation framework - assessing every opportunity against customer value, strategic fit, and business impact - and used it to say no as deliberately as we said yes. Teams were restructured around the company's major growth vectors, each with a dedicated business plan, ensuring the portfolio operated as a connected strategy rather than a collection of independent releases.

Four years later, that cycle of continuous innovation generated over $140M in new revenue, a 5x return on invested capital, and converted a third of the business to a recurring subscription model.
